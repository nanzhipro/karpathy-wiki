# 2025-11-25 — AI & Schools (School Board Talk)

Karpathy's concise position on AI in K-12, distilled from a school-board presentation.

## Four points

### 1. AI detectors don't work and never will

> "You will never be able to detect the use of AI in homework. Full stop. All 'detectors' of AI imo don't really work, can be defeated in various ways, and are in principle doomed to fail. **You have to assume that any work done outside classroom has used AI.**"

Concrete consequence, not aspiration.

### 2. Grading has to move in-class

> "The majority of grading has to shift to in-class work (instead of at-home assignments), in settings where teachers can physically monitor students. The students remain motivated to learn how to solve problems without AI because they know they will be **evaluated without it in class later**."

The lever that makes (1) tractable: decouple *using AI to learn* from *proving you learned without AI*.

### 3. Students should be AI-capable and AI-free

> "We want students to be able to use AI, it is here to stay and it is extremely powerful, but we also don't want students to be **naked in the world without it**."

The calculator analogy:

> "School teaches you how to do all the basic math & arithmetic so that you can in principle do it by hand, even if calculators are pervasive and greatly speed up work in practical settings. In addition, you understand what it's doing for you, so should it give you a wrong answer (e.g. you mistyped 'prompt'), you should be able to notice it, gut check it, verify it."

Critical amplification:

> "**The verification ability is especially important in the case of AI**, which is presently a lot more fallible in a great variety of ways compared to calculators."

This is the [[verification-gap]] argument from Jul 5, now applied to pedagogy.

### 4. Teachers retain creative assessment design space

> "A lot of the evaluation settings remain at teacher's discretion and involve a creative design space of no tools, cheatsheets, open book, provided AI responses, direct internet/AI access, etc."

Nudge toward *menu of evaluation modes*, not one-size-fits-all.

## TL;DR

> "The goal is that the students are **proficient in the use of AI, but can also exist without it**, and imo the only way to get there is to flip classes around and move the majority of testing to in-class settings."

## Why this matters

- **First clear statement of Karpathy's pedagogical stance in the AI era** — concrete, operational, not hand-wavy "critical thinking" platitudes.
- Ports his [[verification-gap]] argument into how schools should assess: grading is discrimination; generation is now cheap.
- Grounds [[ramps-to-knowledge]] — the ramps must still be climbed unaided at least once before the AI can meaningfully leverage them.
- Rejects AI-detector-based policies, which many school districts have implemented. Public, direct, from a figure schools listen to.
- Parallels his longstanding stance on [[physics|physics as mental boot disk]] — AI should be an accelerator on a properly booted brain, not a substitute for booting it.
