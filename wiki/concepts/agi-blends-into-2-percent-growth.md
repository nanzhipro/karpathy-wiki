# "AGI Blends Into 2% Growth"

Karpathy's deliberately unglamorous take on the macroeconomic signature of AGI: the economy will keep growing at roughly **2% per year**, because that growth rate is itself an averaged ceiling of deployment, regulation, and absorption. Transformative technology raises the denominator of what 2% includes; it does not show up as a hockey-stick break in the series.

## Source

[Karpathy, Summoning Ghosts](../sources/summoning-ghosts-not-animals.md) — offered as a calibration against both doomer and accelerationist narratives.

## The argument

1. Historical growth is ~2% in developed economies despite electricity, computing, and the internet.
2. Each of those was "the last invention you'd ever need" in its era — and yet the growth number held.
3. The blocker is not technology; it's the rate at which humans, firms, regulations, and infrastructure can absorb change.
4. AGI, if it arrives, runs into the same bottleneck. Absorption is the rate-limiter.

## What this reframes

- **Fast-takeoff scenarios** over-weight the technology and under-weight the absorption.
- **No-progress scenarios** over-weight the absorption and miss what 2% compounded over decades actually produces.
- **Product-level impact** is enormous and local even when **macro growth** is unchanged.

## Connection to the [[march-of-nines]]

The nines are the microscopic version of the 2% constraint: each successive nine of reliability takes as long as the prior one, which caps deployment pace, which caps the growth impact in any given year.
